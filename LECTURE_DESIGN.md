# Lecture mode - elaborating the direct-instruction scenario

The lecture scenario was just the generic overworld with rows seating, so it played like every
other scene. A real lecture's skill is different: it is the **rhythm of presenting content and
checking for understanding**, keeping a passive audience engaged. This mode makes that the game.

## The core loop (research-grounded)

Each turn the teacher picks one move:

- **Present** - deliver a chunk (Lesson Progress +). But attention DECAYS, and faster the more
  you present in a row (the lecture attention curve; Bligh, *What's the Use of Lectures?*).
  Presenting far ahead of comprehension opens a "confusion gap" (cognitive overload; Sweller).
- **Pose Question** - cold-call a chosen student to check understanding (Lemov, Cold Call;
  Rosenshine 2012, "check for understanding"). Resets the attention curve (accountability /
  group alerting; Kounin), raises comprehension, and reveals if that student is lost. Calling
  must be spread across the room, including the back rows outside the front "action zone"
  (Marx, Fuhrer & Hartig 1999); re-calling the same students is penalized (equity).
- **Wait** - wait time before calling improves the response (Rowe 1986).
- **Re-explain** - address a revealed misconception, closing the confusion gap.
- **Class Check** - a whole-class check (mini-whiteboards / choral response): big comprehension
  and attention boost, reveals everyone, but yields no new content that turn.

## Win / lose

- WIN: deliver the whole lesson (Progress 100%) with Comprehension and Attention still healthy.
- LOSE: Attention or Composure collapses (you lost the room / barreled through and confused them).

## What it teaches (the mismatch it fixes)

A lecture is not "walk around and manage"; it is pacing + frequent checks + equitable
questioning + responsiveness. Spamming Present (no checks) tanks attention and leaves the class
confused; over-checking without progressing stalls momentum. The skill is the rhythm.
